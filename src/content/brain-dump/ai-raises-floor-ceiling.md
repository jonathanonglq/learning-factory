---
title: When AI Raises The Floor But Raises The Ceiling Further
description: A reflection on why Artificial Intelligence may make baseline output easier while giving even more leverage to people who can structure, judge, and adapt.
date: 2026-05-29
---

I was speaking to a QCP intern who was pursuing maths and computer science at Harvard, and he mentioned something that stayed with me: some of his peers do not really know how to write code properly without AI anymore.

I do not think he meant it as a lazy complaint about students these days. These are smart people in a serious environment, using the tools available to them. If AI is already the default layer between intention and implementation, then of course some habits will form around that layer. The part that bothered me was more specific: what happens when AI completes the output even before our internal model of the problem or solution has time to form?

Coding is where the split is easiest to see because the artefact is so concrete. The code either runs or it does not. It fits the existing system or it (quietly) breaks. AI can produce code-shaped output quickly, but robust engineering still depends on knowing what should exist, where it should live, and how it might fail. That is the broader worry. AI makes acceptable output easier to produce, but the people who can frame and judge that output may get even more leverage.

## The Floor Is Real

I do not want to be dismissive about the floor rising. A junior analyst can write an SQL query better than a senior analyst without AI; A non-engineer can build a rough prototype within an hour. In fact, my boss, who has no prior experience with coding (maybe just a bit of R in his university days), just built a fully-functioning multi-agentic workflow webapp to amplify any of his ideas into detailed business proposals over the weekend.

That is genuinely good. Not all friction is noble. Some friction is just bad tooling, missing mentorship, or simply limited headspace. Tool-mediated ability is still ability. We do not usually say someone is fake-productive because they use Excel, Google, libraries, documentation, autocomplete, or Stack Overflow. AI belongs in that lineage, though it is a much stronger version of it.

The question is what happens after the first working version. Raising the floor means more people can reach acceptable. It does not guarantee that more people reach excellent. It also does not guarantee that the gap between acceptable and excellent narrows.

## The Ceiling Moves Differently

The ceiling rises for a different reason. AI does not only help people produce more. It helps people explore more options, test more approaches, read unfamiliar material faster, and compress the distance between idea and artefact. That matters most when the person using it already has some ability to judge the result.

A strong engineer with AI is not merely typing faster. They can sketch multiple architectures, ask the model to attack each one, inspect trade-offs, write tests, and move through unfamiliar code with less drag. A strong analyst can draft queries faster, but the leverage comes from knowing which cut of the data matters in the company's operating environment and whether the result makes sense. A strong writer can generate paragraphs quickly, but the advantage comes from taste, structure, and knowing when a fluent sentence is saying very little.

This is where the title comes from. The floor is the baseline quality of output that more people can now reach. The ceiling is what becomes possible when AI is paired with judgement, domain knowledge, and good taste. My suspicion is that the ceiling may move faster because leverage compounds. Execution gets cheaper, but direction becomes more valuable.

## The Bimodal Shape

I am using bimodal a little loosely here. I do not mean that society will literally settle into a neat two-peak distribution. Surely the world is messier than that. There will be people without good access to AI, people who use it lightly, people who become dependent on it, people who use it as serious leverage, and people whose work is displaced despite being competent.

AI may polarise outcomes even while improving average capability. Two people can produce similar-looking artefacts, but the difference appears only when something breaks, when the prompt gives a plausible but wrong answer, or when the task needs structure.

That split is not only about deep understanding, but also about training environments. If schools and workplaces reward visible output above understanding, people will rationally optimise for visible output. If junior people are not given time to struggle productively, they may not get enough practice to build useful internal models. If managers cannot tell good AI-assisted work from shallow AI-assisted work, the incentives will push in the wrong direction. This is the part I find unsettling. AI makes output easier to produce, but it can also make the quality of the underlying thinking harder to observe.

## The Emotional Weight Of Working Near AI

Honestly, it is hard not to feel a little depressed watching the field move. You spend time trying to understand a tool, a model, a workflow, or a concept properly, and before the understanding feels settled, the frontier shifts again.

It can make skills feel less durable, and parts of a company’s advantage feel more exposed. A product, workflow, or organisational edge that seemed meaningful a year ago can suddenly look thinner if a model makes the execution cheap. The uncomfortable part is that the shelf life of certain forms of competence feels shorter. I think this matters because it shapes our mentality towards learning. If the target keeps moving, the temptation is to skip the slow parts. Why spend time understanding the system if the model can patch over the gap for now? The faster the field moves, the more pressure there is to outsource our internal model just as the tool becomes more powerful.

## The Transition Is Messy (Thankfully?)

Heck, on some days, I think we will all lose our jobs soon, but on other days, I'm convinced that there will still be a role for us just because culture and the way people work are difficult to change. Technology capability and world adoption are not quite the same thing.

Companies do not become AI-native just because the model is available. They need workflows to change. They need data access, security controls, compliance processes (particularly if  it is a regulated company), review mechanisms, cost discipline, training, trust, and management that knows what to do with the tool.

The cost is not only the subscription or inference bill. It is integration work, review time, error correction, workflow disruption, data preparation, legal risk, and the overhead of teaching people how to use the system responsibly. In some cases, AI will pay for itself. In others, the tool may produce activity without much actual productivity.

This transition period is where the ceiling effect may be strongest. The technology is here, but culture, incentives, and operating models are slower. People who understand both AI and the messy human system around it can become unusually valuable. They can translate capability into workflow. They can spot where automation helps, where review is still needed, and where the organisation has changed the surface while leaving the actual process untouched.

That advantage is scoped, though. A person can be excellent with AI and still be blocked by a company that will not redesign work, by incentives that reward the wrong thing, or by a role that gets structurally squeezed. Productivity and bargaining power are not the same.

## Coding Shows The Split Early

This brings me back to the Harvard student. I do not think the real test is whether someone can write code without AI in a blank editor. Good engineers have long used tools, documentation, libraries, autocomplete, search, and examples. There is nothing sacred about typing every line from memory.

The better test is whether someone can reason about the code. What assumptions does it make? Is the abstraction in the right place? What security or performance issue did the model gloss over? If the generated fix passes the happy path, what breaks on the second path?

AI can help people learn these things if they use it actively. It can explain code, propose tests, compare designs, and force alternatives into view. But it can also let someone skip the discomfort that would have taught them the shape of the problem. The tool bridges the gap before the gap becomes visible.

That is why architecture matters more, not less. The scarce skill shifts from writing the function to knowing what function should exist, where it should live, and what could go wrong when it runs, especially if you are dealing with large-scale orchestration systems. It is also taste, which is an annoying word because it sounds vague, but in practice often means knowing what good looks like before the output exists.

## Education Has To Change

Education cannot pretend AI does not exist, but it also cannot treat fundamentals as obsolete just because the surface work can be generated. The hard question is which fundamentals remain load-bearing. Some old exercises may become rituals. Maybe memorising syntax matters less? Maybe some kinds of homework need to change because they now test tool access more than understanding?

Other fundamentals may become more important. In coding, control flow, data structures, debugging, system design, tests, and failure modes still matter. In writing, grammar and polish may matter less, while argument structure, specificity, and judgement matter more.

The point is not to preserve manual work because it is traditional. The point is to preserve the exercises that build internal models, or the ability to think critically. If students learn to produce output without learning how to evaluate it, they may look more capable while becoming more fragile.

## The AGI Boundary

We rattled off on several perspectives so far, but the main argument mostly lives in the transition period before embodied Artificial General Intelligence (AGI) becomes a reality. By embodied, I mean systems that can act in the physical world through robots or other interfaces, not only produce text, code, images, or plans.

During this transition where the ability to harness AI is key, human judgement still matters because institutions, bodies, trust, costs, regulation, and culture move slower than model capability.

If something closer to embodied AGI arrives and can plan, reason, act physically, coordinate, and improve systems better than humans across a wide range of domains, then the argument changes, because AGI would be fully-functioning with no need for human intervention. At this point, the notion of "scarce human skill" would have to be reconsidered.

I believe this will happen some day, but will not try to forecast when. Treating it as a boundary condition feels more useful than trying to forecast it. Before that boundary, where humans still have some control/utility, the immediate question remains more ordinary: does AI strengthen the user’s internal model, or does it let that model decay?

## The New Divide

The divide between people who use AI and people who refuse it already feels stale. The more important and relevant divide is between people and institutions that preserve judgement while automating execution, and those that let their internal model quietly thin out.

AI raises the floor, and for now, it may raise the ceiling further. The distance between those two is where the anxiety sits.
